# AMBITION SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Ambition System defines the medium-term objectives that guide a character's current direction within the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Unlike Dreams, which represent a character's ultimate life aspiration, Ambitions represent concrete goals that can realistically be achieved over months or years.

Ambitions provide momentum.

Dreams provide purpose.

---

# Design Philosophy

Every character should always have something they are actively pursuing.

An Ambition answers the question:

"What is this character trying to accomplish right now?"

Ambitions give direction to daily decisions and naturally generate adventures.

---

# Dream vs Ambition

Dreams and Ambitions are separate systems.

Dream

- Long-term
- Philosophical
- Defines life purpose
- Rarely changes

Ambition

- Medium-term
- Practical
- Defines current objectives
- Changes throughout the campaign

Example

Dream

Become the world's greatest navigator.

Current Ambition

Acquire a ship capable of reaching the Grand Line.

After achieving that goal, a new Ambition naturally replaces it.

---

# Character Creation

Every playable character begins with one Primary Ambition.

The Ambition should support the character's Dream.

It should also fit:

- Background
- Occupation
- Current Situation
- Available Resources
- Personal Relationships

---

# Characteristics of a Good Ambition

A meaningful Ambition is:

- Achievable
- Specific
- Actionable
- Relevant
- Challenging

Examples:

Earn enough money to buy a ship.

Recruit a trustworthy crew.

Become a licensed doctor.

Open a restaurant.

Join the Marines.

Study under a famous shipwright.

Find a Devil Fruit.

Master Observation Haki.

Explore an uncharted island.

---

# Personal Motivation

Every Ambition should answer:

Why is this important now?

Understanding motivation helps both the player and Game Master create meaningful stories.

---

# Multiple Ambitions

Characters may possess several Ambitions.

However,

Only one should be considered the Primary Ambition at any given time.

Additional Ambitions remain secondary.

Too many simultaneous goals reduce narrative focus.

---

# Dynamic Nature

Ambitions naturally evolve.

Examples:

Buying a ship

↓

Finding a crew

↓

Crossing Reverse Mountain

↓

Exploring Paradise

↓

Entering the New World

Each completed Ambition leads naturally toward another.

---

# Failure

Failure does not permanently remove an Ambition.

Characters may:

Try again.

Find another method.

Delay their plans.

Abandon the goal.

Create a new objective.

Failure becomes part of the journey.

---

# Success

Completing an Ambition represents meaningful progress.

Possible rewards include:

New opportunities.

New allies.

Improved Reputation.

Professional recognition.

Personal confidence.

Access to new locations.

Narrative progression.

Completion should feel significant.

---

# Conflict

Ambitions may conflict with:

Dreams.

Relationships.

Crew Goals.

Faction Orders.

Political Events.

Moral Beliefs.

These conflicts create meaningful decisions.

---

# Shared Ambitions

Several characters may pursue the same Ambition.

Examples:

Building a crew.

Defeating a pirate fleet.

Protecting an island.

Opening a business.

Shared Ambitions strengthen cooperation while preserving individual Dreams.

---

# Hidden Ambitions

NPCs possess their own Ambitions.

These goals influence:

Daily behavior.

Long-term planning.

Political decisions.

Professional choices.

Personal relationships.

Players discover NPC ambitions through interaction.

---

# Abandoning an Ambition

Characters may voluntarily abandon an Ambition.

Reasons may include:

Failure.

Changing priorities.

New responsibilities.

Personal growth.

Loss.

Changing an Ambition should always have believable narrative justification.

---

# Relationship With Reputation

Persistent pursuit of an Ambition influences Reputation.

Examples:

A doctor known for treating the poor.

A merchant expanding across multiple islands.

A Marine hunting dangerous pirates.

A pirate challenging stronger opponents.

The world notices determination.

---

# Relationship With Character Growth

Ambitions encourage growth through:

Training.

Travel.

Experience.

Relationships.

Professional development.

Every completed Ambition changes the character in some way.

---

# Relationship With the Living World

The world does not wait.

While a character pursues an Ambition:

Enemies continue acting.

Governments continue changing.

Markets fluctuate.

Wars begin.

Rumors spread.

Characters must adapt their plans to an evolving world.

---

# Game Master Guidelines

The Game Master should use Ambitions to:

Create quests.

Introduce opportunities.

Generate conflicts.

Reward preparation.

Encourage roleplaying.

Develop long-term character arcs.

Ambitions should naturally guide stories rather than dictate them.

---

# Future Expansion

Future Engine modules may introduce:

Crew Ambitions.

Faction Ambitions.

National Ambitions.

Political Ambitions.

Economic Ambitions.

Every expansion must remain compatible with the Core Ambition System.

---

# Engine Principles

The Ambition System follows:

- Story Before System
- Narrative Simulation
- Hidden But Fair
- World Before Player
- No Plot Armor

Dreams define who a character wishes to become.

Ambitions define what they choose to do today.

---

# End of Ambition System

# ATTRIBUTE SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Attribute System defines the innate physical and mental capabilities of every character within the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Attributes represent natural potential rather than learned expertise.

They serve as the foundation for all character actions and interact with Skills, Equipment, Haki, Devil Fruits, Status Effects, and other gameplay systems.

Attributes rarely change over a character's lifetime.

Growth is primarily represented through Skills, Experience, and Narrative Development.

---

# Design Philosophy

Attributes describe what a character is naturally capable of.

Skills describe what a character has learned.

Equipment describes what a character possesses.

Power Systems describe extraordinary abilities.

These systems are intentionally separated.

---

# Core Attribute Model

Every playable character possesses six Core Attributes.

Each attribute represents a broad area of capability rather than a specific statistic.

The six Core Attributes are:

- Strength
- Agility
- Endurance
- Intelligence
- Awareness
- Presence

No additional base attributes exist.

---

# Strength

Strength represents raw physical power.

Strength influences:

- Lifting
- Grappling
- Climbing
- Breaking Objects
- Melee Force
- Physical Labor

Strength does not determine accuracy.

---

# Agility

Agility represents speed, coordination, precision, and balance.

Agility influences:

- Dodging
- Acrobatics
- Stealth Movement
- Balance
- Reflexes
- Fine Motor Control

Agility does not determine endurance.

---

# Endurance

Endurance represents physical resilience.

Endurance influences:

- Stamina
- Fatigue Resistance
- Injury Resistance
- Long-distance Travel
- Survival
- Physical Recovery

Endurance is not equivalent to health points.

---

# Intelligence

Intelligence represents reasoning and accumulated knowledge.

Intelligence influences:

- Investigation
- Learning
- Medicine
- Engineering
- Navigation Theory
- Tactical Planning

Intelligence does not represent wisdom or perception.

---

# Awareness

Awareness represents perception and environmental understanding.

Awareness influences:

- Observation
- Detecting Danger
- Tracking
- Reading Situations
- Searching
- Instinctive Reactions

Awareness supports but does not replace Observation Skills.

---

# Presence

Presence represents force of personality.

Presence influences:

- Leadership
- Persuasion
- Negotiation
- Intimidation
- Inspiration
- Social Confidence

Presence does not guarantee success in conversations.

NPC reactions always depend on context.

---

# Attribute Scale

All Core Attributes use the same rating scale.

| Rating | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Extremely Poor |
| 2 | Poor |
| 3 | Below Average |
| 4 | Average |
| 5 | Above Average |
| 6 | Excellent |
| 7 | Exceptional |
| 8 | Elite |
| 9 | Legendary |
| 10 | World-Class |

Ratings above 10 are not naturally attainable.

---

# Starting Limits

New player characters begin within the limits defined by the Character Creation System.

Characters cannot begin as legendary individuals.

Initial attribute distribution must remain balanced.

The exact allocation method is defined by the Character Creation rules.

---

# Attribute Checks

Whenever an action relies primarily on natural ability, the Game Master determines the most appropriate Attribute.

Examples:

Breaking a locked door

→ Strength

Balancing on a narrow mast during a storm

→ Agility

Marching for several days without rest

→ Endurance

Diagnosing an unfamiliar disease

→ Intelligence

Spotting an ambush before it begins

→ Awareness

Negotiating with a suspicious merchant

→ Presence

---

# Combined Checks

Many actions require both Attributes and Skills.

Examples:

Swordsmanship

→ Agility + Weapon Skill

Emergency Surgery

→ Intelligence + Medical Skill

Ocean Navigation

→ Intelligence + Navigation Skill

Tracking a Fugitive

→ Awareness + Survival Skill

Public Speech

→ Presence + Leadership Skill

The Game Master determines the most appropriate combination.

---

# Attribute Contest

When two characters oppose each other using natural ability, both sides make an appropriate Attribute Check.

Examples:

Arm Wrestling

Strength vs Strength

Chasing on Rooftops

Agility vs Agility

Staring Contest

Presence vs Presence

Holding Breath Underwater

Endurance vs Endurance

The higher successful result gains the advantage.

---

# Attribute Limitations

High Attributes do not guarantee success.

Environmental conditions, injuries, fatigue, fear, equipment, weather, and narrative circumstances may all influence outcomes.

The world remains the primary authority.

---

# Temporary Modifiers

Attributes are normally permanent.

Temporary effects may modify performance without permanently changing the Attribute.

Examples:

- Exhaustion
- Injury
- Poison
- Hunger
- Weather
- Motivation
- Fear

Temporary modifiers expire when their source is resolved.

---

# Permanent Changes

Permanent Attribute changes are extremely rare.

Possible causes include:

- Extraordinary Narrative Events
- Significant Physical Transformation
- Unique Devil Fruit Effects
- Engine-approved Story Milestones

Permanent changes require explicit Game Master approval.

---

# NPC Attributes

NPCs use the same Attribute framework as player characters.

Important NPCs follow the complete Attribute System.

Minor NPCs may use simplified Attribute profiles when appropriate.

---

# Hidden Information

NPC Attributes are hidden from players unless revealed through gameplay.

Players judge opponents through observation, not numerical values.

---

# Engine Principles

The Attribute System follows:

- Story Before System
- Narrative Simulation
- Hidden But Fair
- World Before Player
- No Plot Armor

Attributes define natural potential.

Choices determine how that potential shapes the world.

# BACKGROUND SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Background System defines a character's life before becoming an active participant in the campaign.

A Background explains who the character was before the story begins.

It establishes personal history, education, social standing, early experiences, relationships, opportunities, and worldview.

Backgrounds provide narrative context rather than predetermined destiny.

---

# Design Philosophy

Every individual has a past.

That past shapes:

- Knowledge
- Values
- Habits
- Relationships
- Opportunities
- Fears
- Strengths
- Weaknesses

Backgrounds explain where a character came from.

They do not determine where the character will go.

---

# Narrative Role

Backgrounds exist to answer fundamental questions.

Examples:

Who raised this character?

Where did they grow up?

How were they educated?

What major events shaped them?

Why did they leave home?

What do they believe?

Who remembers them?

The answers create narrative consistency throughout the campaign.

---

# Background Creation

Every playable character must possess one primary Background.

The Background is selected during Character Creation.

The chosen Background becomes a permanent part of the character's history.

---

# Background Components

Every Background contains the following elements.

- Origin
- Childhood
- Education
- Family
- Community
- Early Occupation
- Significant Event
- Starting Relationships
- Starting Reputation
- Personal Values

Each component contributes to the character's identity.

---

# Origin

Origin identifies where the character comes from.

Examples include:

- East Blue
- South Blue
- North Blue
- West Blue
- Paradise
- New World
- Sky Island
- Calm Belt Settlement
- Remote Island

Origin influences cultural familiarity but does not grant mechanical bonuses.

---

# Childhood

Childhood defines the character's early environment.

Examples include:

- Fishing Village
- Busy Port
- Noble Estate
- Marine Base
- Merchant Family
- Traveling Circus
- Mountain Settlement
- Shipyard
- Religious Community

Childhood shapes perspective rather than ability.

---

# Education

Education represents formal and informal learning before the campaign.

Possible sources include:

- Family
- Apprenticeship
- Marine Academy
- Private Tutor
- Self-Education
- Religious Institution
- Local School
- Practical Experience

Education supports believable Skill selection.

---

# Family

Family relationships provide narrative anchors.

Family may include:

- Parents
- Grandparents
- Siblings
- Adoptive Family
- Guardians
- Extended Relatives

Family members remain part of the living world unless otherwise established.

---

# Community

Every character belonged to a community before becoming an adventurer.

Communities influence:

- Reputation
- Customs
- Expectations
- Local Knowledge
- Personal Connections

Communities continue to evolve even after the character leaves.

---

# Early Occupation

Many characters worked before the campaign.

Examples include:

- Fisher
- Doctor
- Carpenter
- Hunter
- Merchant
- Sailor
- Guard
- Farmer
- Blacksmith

Occupation is determined separately within the Occupation System.

Background explains why that occupation was learned.

---

# Significant Event

Every Background contains one defining life event.

Examples:

- Natural disaster
- Pirate attack
- Marine rescue
- Family tragedy
- Great discovery
- Shipwreck
- Political conflict
- Disease outbreak

The event explains why the character's life changed.

---

# Values

Background influences personal values.

Examples:

- Justice
- Freedom
- Wealth
- Honor
- Knowledge
- Family
- Duty
- Adventure

Values influence roleplaying but never remove player agency.

---

# Starting Knowledge

Characters begin with knowledge appropriate to their Background.

Examples:

A fisherman understands local waters.

A doctor recognizes common diseases.

A merchant understands trade customs.

A navigator understands basic charts.

Background knowledge should always remain believable.

---

# Cultural Familiarity

Characters are naturally familiar with the culture in which they were raised.

This familiarity may include:

- Customs
- Dialects
- Food
- Festivals
- Superstitions
- Local Laws

Characters may misunderstand unfamiliar cultures.

---

# Starting Relationships

Background establishes existing relationships.

Examples:

Friends

Teachers

Employers

Family

Childhood Rivals

Former Shipmates

These individuals remain active members of the world.

---

# Starting Reputation

Most characters begin as ordinary people.

Some Backgrounds may provide:

Positive reputation

Negative reputation

Professional recognition

Family reputation

Local notoriety

Reputation never guarantees future treatment.

NPCs judge characters based on current behavior as well as reputation.

---

# Resources

Background may explain access to:

Tools

Books

Property

Family Business

Professional Contacts

Safe Locations

These resources belong to the story rather than the player.

The Game Master determines whether they remain accessible.

---

# Background Secrets

Some Backgrounds contain hidden information.

Examples:

Unknown heritage

Hidden inheritance

Forgotten promises

False identity

Government records

Secret organizations

Players may know or may not know these secrets depending upon the campaign.

The Game Master determines disclosure.

---

# Background Conflicts

A Background should create unresolved narrative opportunities.

Examples:

Missing family member

Debt

Broken friendship

Former mentor

Failed promise

Old enemy

These conflicts generate future stories.

---

# Changing Background

A character's Background never changes.

However,

Its meaning may change as new information is revealed.

Characters discover new truths about their own past throughout the campaign.

---

# Interaction With Other Systems

Background interacts with:

Character Creation

Occupation

Skills

Relationships

Reputation

Dreams

Ambitions

Lifestyle

NPC System

Faction System

World System

Every interaction must remain logically consistent.

---

# Game Master Guidelines

The Game Master should use Backgrounds to:

Introduce story hooks.

Create believable NPC connections.

Generate emotional investment.

Reward thoughtful character creation.

Expand the living world.

Background should never be ignored after Session 0.

---

# Engine Principles

The Background System follows:

Story Before System.

Narrative Simulation.

Hidden But Fair.

World Before Player.

No Plot Armor.

Every character begins the story with a past.

That past continues to shape the future long after the adventure begins.

---

# End of Background System

# CHARACTER CREATION SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Character Creation System defines the standardized process for creating a new playable character.

Character creation establishes a character's identity, history, motivations, and starting capabilities while ensuring compatibility with the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Every player character begins as an ordinary individual living within the world, not as a legendary figure.

---

# Design Philosophy

Character creation prioritizes narrative identity over mechanical optimization.

A compelling character is defined by:

- Personality
- Goals
- Relationships
- Strengths
- Weaknesses
- Life Experience

Statistics exist to support storytelling, not replace it.

---

# Character Creation Timing

New characters are created during:

- Session 0
- Character Replacement
- Approved Story Introduction

Characters are never generated during active scenes unless required by the Game Master.

---

# Character Creation Workflow

Every character follows the same creation sequence.

1. Character Concept

↓

2. Basic Identity

↓

3. Background

↓

4. Occupation

↓

5. Attributes

↓

6. Skills

↓

7. Traits

↓

8. Flaws

↓

9. Dream

↓

10. Ambition

↓

11. Relationships

↓

12. Starting Equipment

↓

13. Starting Reputation

↓

14. Final Review

↓

Character Approved

---

# Character Concept

The player first defines a simple concept.

Examples:

- Honest Marine Recruit
- Traveling Doctor
- Former Shipwright
- Treasure Hunter
- Wandering Musician
- Island Hunter
- Merchant Bodyguard

The concept guides all later decisions.

---

# Basic Identity

Every character records:

- Name
- Age
- Gender
- Height
- Appearance
- Birth Island
- Native Sea
- Dominant Hand
- Languages
- Distinguishing Features

Identity provides narrative context only unless otherwise specified.

---

# Background

Background represents the character's early life before gameplay.

Background influences:

- Knowledge
- Starting Relationships
- Initial Opportunities
- Narrative Hooks

Background does not guarantee future success.

---

# Occupation

Occupation represents the character's profession before joining the story.

Examples include:

- Fisher
- Cook
- Carpenter
- Sailor
- Doctor
- Hunter
- Merchant
- Guard

Occupation grants appropriate starting experience and skills.

---

# Attributes

Core Attributes define the character's natural capabilities.

Attributes are assigned according to the Attribute System.

No attribute may exceed the starting limits defined by the engine.

---

# Skills

Skills represent learned experience.

Skills originate from:

- Background
- Occupation
- Training
- Life Experience

Skill assignment follows the Skill System.

---

# Traits

Traits describe positive personal qualities.

Examples:

- Calm
- Brave
- Curious
- Patient
- Observant
- Charismatic

Traits influence narrative interpretation and specific mechanics where applicable.

---

# Flaws

Every character must possess meaningful flaws.

Examples:

- Reckless
- Proud
- Impatient
- Gullible
- Cowardly
- Stubborn

Flaws create dramatic opportunities and meaningful decisions.

---

# Dream

Every playable character possesses one major dream.

Examples:

- Discover a legendary island
- Cure every known disease
- Build the strongest ship
- Become the greatest navigator
- Protect a homeland

Dreams provide long-term motivation.

Dreams may evolve through play.

---

# Ambition

Ambition defines the character's immediate direction.

Unlike dreams, ambitions are achievable within shorter timeframes.

Examples:

- Earn enough money for a ship
- Join a famous crew
- Become a Marine Officer
- Learn Haki
- Find a skilled mentor

Ambitions change throughout the campaign.

---

# Relationships

Characters begin with meaningful social connections.

Relationships may include:

- Family
- Friends
- Rivals
- Teachers
- Employers
- Former Crewmates

Relationships exist independently within the world.

---

# Starting Equipment

Characters receive starting equipment based on:

- Occupation
- Background
- Story Circumstances

Equipment is intentionally modest.

Legendary equipment is never available during character creation.

---

# Starting Reputation

New characters begin with an initial reputation determined by their history.

Most characters begin as unknown individuals.

Exceptional backgrounds may grant minor recognition at the Game Master's discretion.

---

# Character Validation

Before approval, every character must satisfy all engine requirements.

Validation confirms:

- Complete Information
- Legal Attribute Allocation
- Legal Skill Allocation
- Valid Background
- Valid Occupation
- Required Trait
- Required Flaw
- Dream Defined
- Ambition Defined

Incomplete characters cannot enter play.

---

# Character Approval

The Game Master performs the final review.

Approval ensures:

- Narrative Consistency
- Mechanical Balance
- World Compatibility

Approved characters officially become part of the living world.

---

# Replacement Characters

Replacement characters follow the same creation process.

Existing world events remain unchanged.

Replacement characters enter the story naturally through narrative circumstances.

---

# Engine Principles

The Character Creation System follows:

- Story Before System
- Narrative Simulation
- Hidden But Fair
- World Before Player
- No Plot Armor

Character creation establishes the beginning of a life within the world, not the beginning of the world itself.

# CHARACTER GROWTH SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Character Growth System defines how a character develops throughout the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Growth is not measured solely by increased capability.

It also represents changes in personality, knowledge, relationships, reputation, responsibilities, and worldview.

A stronger character is not always a more mature character.

---

# Design Philosophy

Characters grow because they live.

Every challenge leaves an impact.

Every victory teaches confidence.

Every failure teaches humility.

Every relationship changes perspective.

Growth reflects experience rather than numerical progression.

---

# Growth Philosophy

Character Growth represents long-term personal development.

Growth should feel:

- Earned
- Believable
- Meaningful
- Gradual
- Permanent

There are no instant transformations.

---

# Sources of Growth

Characters grow through:

- Adventure
- Failure
- Success
- Training
- Leadership
- Responsibility
- Friendship
- Rivalry
- Loss
- Discovery
- Sacrifice
- Survival

Meaningful experiences create meaningful growth.

---

# Areas of Growth

Growth may occur in many different areas.

Examples include:

Physical Growth

Mental Growth

Emotional Growth

Professional Growth

Social Growth

Leadership Growth

Moral Growth

Philosophical Growth

A character rarely grows in every area equally.

---

# Physical Growth

Physical Growth reflects long-term changes resulting from training and experience.

Examples:

Improved endurance.

Greater strength.

Better coordination.

Higher resilience.

Physical Growth supports future Attribute development where appropriate.

---

# Mental Growth

Mental Growth represents increasing knowledge and understanding.

Examples:

Better judgment.

Improved planning.

Greater adaptability.

Expanded education.

Better problem solving.

---

# Emotional Growth

Characters learn to understand themselves.

Examples:

Managing fear.

Accepting failure.

Forgiving others.

Developing confidence.

Overcoming trauma.

Emotional Growth often changes how characters approach difficult situations.

---

# Professional Growth

Professional Growth reflects mastery within an Occupation.

Examples:

Doctor becoming a respected surgeon.

Navigator mastering dangerous seas.

Shipwright designing larger vessels.

Merchant managing international trade.

Professional Growth naturally influences Reputation.

---

# Leadership Growth

Leadership develops through responsibility.

Examples:

Commanding a crew.

Resolving conflict.

Making difficult decisions.

Protecting others.

Accepting accountability.

Leadership cannot be learned through theory alone.

---

# Social Growth

Relationships shape people.

Characters grow through:

Friendship.

Conflict.

Mentorship.

Family.

Romance.

Cooperation.

Repeated interaction changes people over time.

---

# Moral Growth

Experiences challenge personal beliefs.

Examples:

Justice.

Freedom.

Duty.

Compassion.

Revenge.

Mercy.

Characters may reinforce or change their values.

---

# Growth Through Failure

Failure is one of the greatest sources of development.

Failure may teach:

Preparation.

Patience.

Humility.

Determination.

Creativity.

Failure should never be treated as wasted progress.

---

# Growth Through Success

Success also changes characters.

Possible effects include:

Confidence.

Responsibility.

Recognition.

New expectations.

Greater influence.

Success should create new challenges rather than ending character development.

---

# Relationships and Growth

Relationships are powerful catalysts.

Friends.

Rivals.

Teachers.

Students.

Crewmates.

Family.

Every meaningful relationship has the potential to change a character.

---

# Dreams and Growth

Dreams provide long-term direction.

As characters pursue their Dreams,

their personalities,

beliefs,

and priorities naturally evolve.

Dreams inspire growth.

Growth reshapes Dreams.

---

# Ambitions and Growth

Completing Ambitions creates momentum.

Each completed objective prepares the character for greater challenges.

Growth follows accomplishment,

not completion alone.

---

# Reputation and Growth

Public recognition changes opportunities.

Fame.

Infamy.

Respect.

Fear.

These reactions influence future decisions and responsibilities.

---

# Setbacks

Growth is rarely linear.

Characters may experience:

Loss.

Failure.

Fear.

Disillusionment.

Burnout.

These setbacks create opportunities for future development.

---

# Permanent Change

Some experiences permanently change a character.

Examples:

Life-changing injury.

Loss of a mentor.

Discovery of hidden truth.

Leadership during war.

Near-death experience.

Permanent changes should always arise naturally from the story.

---

# Player Agency

The player decides how their character grows.

The Game Master presents situations.

The player's choices determine the outcome.

Growth cannot be forced.

---

# NPC Growth

NPCs continue growing independently.

Friends become leaders.

Rivals become stronger.

Children become adults.

Governments evolve.

The world never remains static.

# Interaction With Other Systems

Character Growth interacts with:

Attributes.

Skills.

Traits.

Flaws.

Dreams.

Ambitions.

Reputation.

Relationships.

Lifestyle.

Experience.

Every system contributes to personal development.

---

# Game Master Guidelines

The Game Master should encourage growth through:

Meaningful choices.

Consequences.

Long-term storytelling.

Personal conflict.

Emotional moments.

Growth should emerge naturally from play rather than scripted events.

---

# Engine Principles

The Character Growth System follows:

Story Before System.

Narrative Simulation.

Hidden But Fair.

World Before Player.

No Plot Armor.

A character's greatest growth is measured not by power,

but by the person they become.

# DREAM SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Dream System defines the ultimate aspiration that drives a character throughout their life.

A Dream represents the highest goal a character wishes to achieve.

It is the emotional and philosophical foundation of the character's journey.

Dreams give purpose to adventure.

Without a Dream,

a character merely reacts to the world.

With a Dream,

a character actively shapes their own future.

---

# Design Philosophy

Every great journey begins with a reason.

Power.

Wealth.

Knowledge.

Freedom.

Justice.

Family.

Adventure.

Discovery.

Each Dream represents something the character values above ordinary life.

Dreams are not objectives assigned by the Game Master.

They belong entirely to the character.

---

# Dream Definition

A Dream is:

- Long-term
- Personal
- Meaningful
- Difficult
- Inspirational

Dreams should require years of effort.

Some Dreams may never be fully completed.

That possibility makes them meaningful.

---

# Character Creation

Every playable character begins with one Primary Dream.

The Dream is selected during Character Creation.

It should naturally fit the character's:

- Background
- Values
- Personality
- Occupation
- Life Experiences

Dreams should never be selected for mechanical advantages.

---

# Characteristics of a Good Dream

A meaningful Dream is:

Specific.

Believable.

Motivating.

Narratively interesting.

Capable of creating difficult choices.

Examples:

Become the greatest shipwright in the world.

Discover every island in the Grand Line.

Develop a cure for every known disease.

Protect every village in my homeland.

Build a fleet capable of crossing any sea.

Create the world's finest restaurant.

Reveal the truth behind forgotten history.

---

# Poorly Defined Dreams

Dreams should avoid being:

Too vague.

Too easy.

Entirely dependent upon another character.

Purely mechanical.

Examples:

Become stronger.

Earn money.

Win battles.

Travel somewhere.

These goals may support a Dream,

but should not replace one.

---

# Personal Motivation

A Dream should answer:

Why does this matter?

Personal motivation creates emotional investment.

Different characters may share the same Dream,

yet pursue it for entirely different reasons.

---

# Dream and Roleplaying

Dreams influence:

Decision Making.

Risk Assessment.

Relationships.

Leadership.

Personal Sacrifice.

Long-Term Planning.

The player remains free to ignore their Dream.

Doing so becomes part of the character's story.

---

# Dream Conflicts

Dreams may conflict with:

Other Dreams.

Crew Goals.

Faction Objectives.

Political Events.

Personal Relationships.

Survival.

# EXPERIENCE SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Experience System records the meaningful events that shape a character's development throughout the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Experience represents what a character has truly lived through.

It is not a numerical reward.

It is accumulated through meaningful action, difficult decisions, success, failure, and survival.

Experience explains why a character is capable of growth.

---

# Design Philosophy

Characters become stronger because they experience life.

Experience cannot be purchased.

It cannot be granted arbitrarily.

It cannot be farmed through meaningless repetition.

Growth requires genuine experience.

---

# Experience Definition

Experience represents accumulated knowledge gained through direct participation in the world.

Experience may come from:

- Adventure
- Work
- Training
- Leadership
- Exploration
- Failure
- Success
- Loss
- Discovery
- Conflict
- Survival
- Teaching

Every meaningful event contributes to the character's understanding of the world.

---

# Meaningful Experience

Only meaningful experiences contribute to long-term growth.

Examples include:

Surviving a dangerous voyage.

Performing difficult surgery.

Leading a crew through disaster.

Negotiating peace between rival factions.

Escaping imprisonment.

Discovering an unknown island.

Repeated routine tasks eventually lose educational value.

---

# Routine Experience

Routine activities remain part of everyday life.

Examples:

Preparing ordinary meals.

Repairing familiar equipment.

Sailing calm waters.

Patrolling peaceful streets.

Routine work maintains proficiency but rarely produces significant growth.

---

# Learning Through Failure

Failure provides valuable experience.

Characters often learn more from mistakes than success.

Examples:

A failed operation improves future medical judgment.

A lost battle teaches strategy.

A navigation mistake teaches caution.

Failure should always create opportunities for future improvement.

---

# Learning Through Success

Success reinforces effective decisions.

Examples:

Successfully commanding a ship.

Completing a difficult construction project.

Winning negotiations.

Discovering hidden knowledge.

Success demonstrates mastery but should continue creating new challenges.

---

# Training

Training is a legitimate source of Experience.

Effective training requires:

Time.

Discipline.

Appropriate conditions.

Meaningful effort.

Qualified instruction when available.

Training complements real-world experience.

It does not replace it.

---

# Teaching

Teaching others strengthens personal understanding.

Experienced characters often deepen their own knowledge while instructing students.

Teaching requires:

Knowledge.

Communication.

Patience.

Practical demonstration.

---

# Shared Experience

Groups often grow together.

Examples:

Crew members surviving storms.

Medical teams responding to disasters.

Marines defending a fortress.

Explorers discovering new lands.

Shared experiences strengthen relationships while supporting individual growth.

---

# Extraordinary Experience

Rare events may produce extraordinary personal development.

Examples:

Near-death experiences.

Witnessing historical events.

Discovering ancient civilizations.

Leading a revolution.

Surviving the Grand Line.

Such events permanently influence the character's outlook.

---

# Experience and Skills

Experience creates opportunities for Skill improvement.

Experience alone does not automatically increase Skill Ratings.

Skill advancement remains governed by the Skill System.

---

# Experience and Attributes

Experience may justify rare Attribute growth.

However,

most Attribute changes require extraordinary narrative justification.

Experience supports potential.

It does not guarantee transformation.

---

# Experience and Dreams

Pursuing a Dream naturally generates valuable experiences.

Every step toward a Dream shapes the character regardless of ultimate success.

---

# Experience and Ambitions

Completing Ambitions often provides the experience necessary to pursue even greater objectives.

Each achievement prepares the character for future challenges.

---

# Experience and Reputation

Extraordinary experiences often become public stories.

However,

many important experiences remain unknown to the world.

Personal growth and public recognition are separate concepts.

---

# Experience and Memory

Characters remember important experiences.

Past events influence:

Decision making.

Emotional responses.

Relationships.

Professional judgment.

Future ambitions.

Experience creates wisdom.

---

# Experience Loss

Experience cannot normally be lost.

Knowledge remains even after failure.

Characters may lose confidence,

resources,

or opportunities,

but lived experience remains part of who they are.

---

# NPC Experience

NPCs continue gaining experience independently.

Veteran sailors become better navigators.

Young officers become experienced commanders.

Doctors improve through years of practice.

The world grows alongside the players.

---

# Game Master Guidelines

The Game Master should reward meaningful experiences by:

Creating new opportunities.

Allowing believable growth.

Recognizing long-term effort.

Supporting character development.

Experience should never become a currency.

# Interaction With Other Systems

The Experience System supports:

Character Growth.

Skills.

Dreams.

Ambitions.

Occupations.

Reputation.

Relationships.

Lifestyle.

---

# Engine Principles

The Experience System follows:

Story Before System.

Narrative Simulation.

Hidden But Fair.

World Before Player.

No Plot Armor.

# FLAW SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Flaw System defines the persistent weaknesses, limitations, and personal shortcomings that shape a character throughout the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Every playable character possesses flaws.

No individual is without weakness.

Flaws create believable characters, meaningful conflicts, and opportunities for growth.

---

# Design Philosophy

Perfection does not exist.

Every strength creates limitations.

Every virtue has a cost.

Flaws are not punishments.

They are narrative tools that create drama, tension, difficult decisions, and personal growth.

A memorable character is defined as much by their weaknesses as by their strengths.

---

# Flaw Definition

A Flaw is a persistent characteristic that regularly creates challenges for the character.

Flaws are:

- Personal
- Consistent
- Believable
- Imperfect
- Narratively meaningful

Flaws are not:

- Temporary emotions
- Injuries
- Diseases
- Status Effects
- Lack of Skill

---

# Character Creation

Every playable character must possess at least one meaningful Flaw.

Additional Flaws are allowed when supported by the character concept.

Flaws should naturally arise from:

- Background
- Childhood
- Trauma
- Education
- Occupation
- Personal History

---

# Narrative Purpose

Flaws exist to create:

- Internal conflict
- Difficult choices
- Personal struggle
- Relationship tension
- Character development
- Story opportunities

The purpose of a Flaw is never to punish the player.

---

# Categories

Flaws generally belong to one or more of the following categories.

---

## Emotional Flaws

Examples:

- Impatient
- Jealous
- Proud
- Cowardly
- Hot-Tempered
- Overprotective
- Stubborn
- Insecure

---

## Social Flaws

Examples:

- Distrustful
- Arrogant
- Blunt
- Socially Awkward
- Naive
- Judgmental
- Easily Influenced

---

## Mental Flaws

Examples:

- Overthinking
- Impulsive
- Forgetful
- Indecisive
- Obsessive
- Easily Distracted

---

## Professional Flaws

Examples:

- Careless
- Perfectionist
- Disorganized
- Risk-Taking
- Overconfident
- Hesitant

---

## Moral Flaws

Examples:

- Greedy
- Vengeful
- Ruthless
- Self-Centered
- Dishonest
- Manipulative

---

# Narrative Influence

Flaws naturally influence:

Decision Making.

Relationships.

Leadership.

Reputation.

Stress Responses.

Professional Conduct.

The Game Master interprets these influences through narrative.

---

# Player Agency

Players always retain control of their characters.

A Flaw never forces an action.

Instead,

it suggests believable tendencies.

The player may choose to resist a Flaw,

accept it,

or struggle against it.

That struggle creates compelling stories.

---

# Internal Conflict

Characters often possess Traits and Flaws that oppose one another.

Examples:

Brave

+

Impulsive

Kind

+

Overprotective

Analytical

+

Perfectionist

Confident

+

Arrogant

These conflicts create believable personalities.

---

# Consequences

Ignoring a Flaw may have no consequence.

Following a Flaw may create:

Complications.

New Opportunities.

Relationship Changes.

Unexpected Problems.

Interesting Stories.

The Game Master should prefer consequences over penalties.

---

# Flaw Evolution

Flaws may evolve through long-term character development.

Possible outcomes include:

Acceptance.

Improvement.

Transformation.

Replacement.

Worsening.

Development must always occur through narrative events,

never through automatic advancement.

---

# Replacing a Flaw

Replacing a Flaw requires:

Major personal growth.

Long-term roleplaying.

Meaningful story events.

Game Master approval.

The original Flaw remains part of the character's history.

---

# Hidden Flaws

NPCs possess their own Flaws.

Players discover them through:

Observation.

Conversation.

Rumors.

Shared experiences.

No individual fully understands another person at first meeting.

---

# Relationships

Flaws strongly influence long-term relationships.

Examples:

A stubborn captain may frustrate the crew.

An arrogant doctor may lose patient trust.

A jealous rival may become dangerous.

A naive merchant may attract scammers.

Relationships evolve naturally through repeated interaction.

---

# Reputation

Repeated demonstrations of a Flaw may influence Reputation.

Examples:

Greed.

Cowardice.

Cruelty.

Dishonesty.

Impulsiveness.

Reputation reflects observed behavior,

not hidden personality.

---

# Flaw and Skills

Flaws never directly reduce Skill Ratings.

Instead,

they influence how Skills are used.

Example:

An impulsive surgeon may rush procedures.

A perfectionist shipwright may work more slowly.

An arrogant negotiator may underestimate others.

The Skill remains unchanged.

Behavior changes.

# Flaw and Attributes

Flaws are independent from Attributes.

A powerful warrior may still be insecure.

A brilliant scientist may be arrogant.

A charismatic leader may be selfish.

Each system serves a different purpose.

# Game Master Guidelines

Generate emotional conflict.

Develop long-term stories.

# Future Expansion

Cultural Flaws.

# Engine Principles

# PLAYER SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Player System defines how a player exists inside the Narrative Simulation Engine.

A player controls one Character.

The player influences only the decisions of that character.

The player never controls:

- NPCs
- World Events
- Politics
- Other Crews
- Time
- Random Outcomes

The world exists independently of the player.

---

# Design Philosophy

The Player is a participant inside a living world.

The engine does not adapt itself around players.

Players must adapt to the world.

Every action has consequences.

Every choice creates new possibilities while closing others.

---

# Player Authority

Players may decide:

- Movement
- Speech
- Combat Decisions
- Tactical Choices
- Skill Usage
- Equipment Usage
- Social Interactions
- Long-Term Goals
- Crew Discussions

Players may not decide:

- Hidden Information
- NPC Intentions
- Dice Results
- World Events
- Secret Systems
- Weather
- Political Decisions
- Enemy Strategies

---

# Character Ownership

Each player permanently owns one playable character unless:

- Character Death
- Retirement
- Story Exit
- Character Transfer approved by the Game Master

Ownership includes:

- Personality
- Decisions
- Personal Goals
- Relationships
- Growth

Ownership does not include immunity from consequences.

---

# Player Knowledge vs Character Knowledge

The engine always separates:

## Player Knowledge

Information known by the human player.

## Character Knowledge

Information the character has actually learned.

The Game Master always resolves actions using Character Knowledge.

Metagaming is not permitted.

---

# Roleplaying Standard

Players should make decisions according to:

- Personality
- Values
- Knowledge
- Emotional State
- Current Situation

Optimal decisions are not required.

Believable decisions are preferred.

---

# Player Intent

Before resolving uncertain actions the Game Master determines:

Intent

Examples:

"I want to scare him."

"I want to escape."

"I want to protect civilians."

"I want to gather information."

Intent determines:

- Dice
- Difficulty
- Possible Outcomes
- Narrative Consequences

---

# Decision Before Roll

The engine follows this sequence:

Intent

↓

Approach

↓

Difficulty

↓

Roll

↓

Narrative Result

Players never roll before declaring intent.

---

# Failure Philosophy

Failure is content.

Failure creates:

- New Problems
- New Opportunities
- Character Growth

# RELATIONSHIP SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Relationship System defines how characters are connected to other individuals within the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Relationships represent emotional bonds, history, trust, conflict, and shared experience.

They are one of the primary forces shaping character behavior and world interaction.

---

# Design Philosophy

No character exists in isolation.

Every individual is part of a network of connections.

Relationships influence:

- Decisions
- Trust
- Loyalty
- Conflict
- Cooperation
- Emotional responses
- Long-term story development

Relationships are not static statistics.

They are living narrative bonds.

---

# Relationship Definition

A Relationship represents the dynamic connection between two characters.

It is defined by:

- History
- Emotional tone
- Trust level
- Shared experiences
- Current status

Relationships evolve naturally through gameplay.

---

# Core Relationship Types

Relationships are categorized into narrative types rather than numerical labels.

---

## Positive Relationships

Examples:

Friend

Close Friend

Best Friend

Ally

Trusted Companion

Mentor

Student

Family Member

Loyal Crewmate

Positive relationships encourage cooperation and support.

---

## Neutral Relationships

Examples:

Acquaintance

Stranger

Professional Contact

Trade Partner

Neutral relationships are unstable and can evolve in any direction.

---

## Negative Relationships

Examples:

Rival

Enemy

Distrusted Individual

Former Ally

Betrayer

Negative relationships create tension and conflict.

---

## Complex Relationships

Some relationships combine multiple emotional layers.

Examples:

Rival with Respect

Former Friend

Reluctant Ally

Enemy with Shared History

Love-Hate Relationship

These are common in long-term narratives.

---

# Trust

Trust is a core element of relationships.

Trust influences:

- Willingness to cooperate
- Information sharing
- Risk-taking
- Loyalty during conflict

Trust is earned slowly and lost quickly.

---

# History

Shared history defines the depth of a relationship.

Examples:

Saved someone’s life.

Fought together in battle.

Traveled together.

Shared hardship.

Betrayal.

History cannot be ignored in future interactions.

---

# Emotional Tone

Each relationship carries an emotional tone.

Examples:

Warm

Cold

Hostile

Respectful

Distant

Protective

Emotional tone affects dialogue and behavior.

---

# Relationship Development

Relationships evolve through:

Shared experiences.

Conflict resolution.

Cooperation.

Betrayal.

Communication.

Distance or separation.

Absence may weaken relationships over time.

---

# Relationship Decay

Relationships may weaken if:

Characters stop interacting.

Trust is broken.

Goals diverge.

Conflicts remain unresolved.

Decay does not erase history,

but it changes emotional tone.

---

# Relationship Growth

Positive interactions strengthen relationships.

Examples:

Helping in danger.

Keeping promises.

Sharing resources.

Supporting goals.

Protecting each other.

Strong relationships create powerful narrative alliances.

---

# Betrayal

Betrayal permanently alters relationships.

Consequences include:

Loss of trust.

Reputation damage.

Faction consequences.

Emotional trauma.

Some relationships can never fully recover.

---

# Romance

Romantic relationships are possible but not required.

Romance follows the same system rules:

Trust.

History.

Shared experience.

Emotional development.

Romance evolves naturally from interaction,

not from mechanical assignment.

---

# Family Relationships

Family ties are special narrative connections.

They may include:

Parents.

Siblings.

Children.

Guardians.

Family relationships often persist regardless of distance or conflict.

---

# Mentor and Student

Mentorship is a structured relationship based on learning.

Mentors influence:

Skills.

Values.

Career development.

Worldview.

Students may eventually surpass mentors.

---

# Rivalry

Rivalries are competitive relationships.

They may be:

Friendly.

Hostile.

Respectful.

Obsessive.

Rivals often drive personal growth.

---

# Crew Relationships

Crewmates form one of the strongest relationship structures.

Crew bonds include:

Trust.

Shared survival.

Mutual responsibility.

Shared goals.

Crew relationships are central in maritime narratives.

---

# Hidden Relationships

Not all relationships are known immediately.

NPCs may:

Hide intentions.

Fake friendship.

Conceal hatred.

Secretly observe others.

Truth is discovered through interaction.

---

# Public vs Private Relationships

Public perception may differ from reality.

Example:

Two individuals publicly appear as allies but secretly distrust each other.

Or

Enemies publicly but secretly cooperating.

Both layers exist simultaneously.

---

# Relationship Influence on Behavior

Relationships directly affect NPC and character behavior.

Examples:

Trusted allies may provide help without request.

Enemies may act preemptively.

Family may intervene in conflicts.

Rivals may appear at critical moments.

---

# Relationship and Reputation Interaction

Relationships and Reputation are separate systems.

Reputation affects first impressions.

Relationships determine personal behavior.

A famous hero may still be disliked personally.

A feared criminal may have loyal friends.

---

# Relationship and Factions

Factions influence relationships between members.

Examples:

Marines may distrust pirates.

Revolutionaries may oppose government officials.

Merchant guilds may prefer stability.

Faction alignment influences but does not determine relationships.

---

# Game Master Guidelines

The Game Master should use Relationships to:

Create emotional depth.

Drive character decisions.

Generate conflict.

Support long-term storytelling.

Build immersive NPC networks.

Relationships should evolve naturally from gameplay.

Never assign or remove relationships arbitrarily.

---

# Future Expansion

Future systems may introduce:

Crew Bond System.

Family Tree System.

Romance Expansion System.

Faction Diplomacy System.

Political Relationship System.

All expansions must remain compatible with the Core Relationship System.

---

# Engine Principles

The Relationship System follows:

Story Before System.

Narrative Simulation.

Hidden But Fair.

World Before Player.

No Plot Armor.

Every bond is shaped by shared experience.

Every connection tells a story.

# REPUTATION SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Reputation System tracks how the world perceives a character.

Reputation is earned through actions, achievements, failures, rumors, and the spread of information.

It exists independently of the character's personal beliefs.

A character may believe themselves to be a hero while the world views them as a criminal.

Likewise, a feared pirate may secretly perform acts of kindness unknown to the public.

Reputation represents perception, not truth.

---

# Design Philosophy

The world remembers.

People talk.

Witnesses survive.

Newspapers publish stories.

Governments investigate.

Pirates exaggerate.

Rumors spread.

Every meaningful action leaves an impression.

Reputation ensures that actions continue to influence the world long after they occur.

---

# Reputation Principles

Reputation is:

- Earned.
- Dynamic.
- Localized.
- Imperfect.
- Persistent.

Reputation is never assigned simply because the player desires it.

---

# Sources of Reputation

A character's Reputation may change through:

- Heroic actions.
- Criminal activity.
- Public speeches.
- Professional excellence.
- Acts of cruelty.
- Rescue missions.
- Military victories.
- Political influence.
- Scientific discoveries.
- Exploration.
- Journalism.
- Commerce.
- Rumors.
- Betrayal.
- Sacrifice.

Both success and failure may affect Reputation.

---

# Public Knowledge

The public rarely knows the complete truth.

Instead,

people remember stories.

Examples:

"The captain defeated three pirate crews."

"The doctor saved an entire village."

"The merchant cheated the governor."

Whether these stories are accurate is often irrelevant.

Public perception shapes Reputation.

---

# Reputation Scale

Reputation exists on a narrative scale rather than a fixed numerical value.

Common stages include:

Unknown

↓

Recognized

↓

Well Known

↓

Famous

↓

Renowned

↓

Legendary

Recognition may be positive,

negative,

or mixed.

---

# Local Reputation

Reputation is regional.

A hero in East Blue may be completely unknown in Paradise.

Likewise,

a notorious pirate in the New World may attract little attention in a remote village.

Information spreads gradually.

---

# Professional Reputation

Professional Reputation develops independently.

Examples:

A famous surgeon.

A respected navigator.

A renowned shipwright.

A legendary chef.

Professional reputation creates opportunities within the character's field.

---

# Criminal Reputation

Illegal actions may create Criminal Reputation.

Examples:

Piracy.

Smuggling.

Assassination.

Fraud.

Prison escape.

Government opposition.

Criminal Reputation attracts attention from law enforcement and rival criminals alike.

---

# Government Reputation

Governments maintain records on individuals of interest.

Records may include:

Criminal history.

Military service.

Professional licenses.

Political activity.

Known associates.

These records are not always public.

---

# Faction Reputation

Each Faction forms its own opinion of a character.

Examples:

Marines.

World Government.

Pirate Crews.

Revolutionary Army.

Merchant Guilds.

Independent Nations.

Improving Reputation with one Faction may reduce it with another.

Relationships between Factions influence these changes.

---

# Personal Reputation

NPCs remember personal experiences.

Examples:

Promises kept.

Promises broken.

Acts of kindness.

Acts of cruelty.

Professional competence.

Leadership.

Personal Reputation spreads slowly but remains highly reliable.

---

# Reputation Spread

Information spreads through:

Witnesses.

Travelers.

Merchants.

Newspapers.

Den Den Mushi.

Government Reports.

Pirate Rumors.

Word of mouth.

The speed of information depends upon:

Distance.

Importance.

Political interest.

Media coverage.

Communication networks.

---

# False Reputation

Not all Reputation is accurate.

Examples:

Mistaken identity.

False accusations.

Deliberate propaganda.

Exaggerated stories.

Hidden heroism.

# SKILL SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Skill System defines the learned abilities of every character within the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Unlike Attributes, Skills represent knowledge, training, practice, and experience gained throughout life.

Skills are the primary method by which characters improve over time.

Every meaningful profession, combat style, craft, and specialization is represented through Skills.

---

# Design Philosophy

Attributes determine natural potential.

Skills determine trained capability.

A naturally talented individual without training can still be surpassed by someone with years of experience.

The engine rewards learning rather than innate superiority.

---

# Skill Principles

Skills represent:

- Education
- Practice
- Professional Experience
- Repeated Success
- Repeated Failure
- Muscle Memory
- Technical Knowledge

Skills never represent supernatural power.

Devil Fruits and Haki are independent systems.

---

# Skill Categories

Every Skill belongs to one primary category.

Categories organize similar abilities but provide no mechanical bonus.

Categories include:

# Skill Resolution System

The Skill Resolution System defines how Skills are evaluated during gameplay.

Every Skill Check follows the same fundamental process regardless of the Skill involved.

Consistency is more important than speed.

The Game Master always applies the same reasoning to similar situations.

---

# Resolution Philosophy

A Skill Check answers one question:

"Can this character successfully accomplish this action under the current circumstances?"

Skill Checks are not performed simply because a player attempts an action.

Dice are only introduced when:

- Success is uncertain.
- Failure is meaningful.
- Multiple outcomes are possible.

If success or failure is obvious, no roll is required.

---

# Resolution Sequence

Every Skill Check follows the same sequence.

Step 1

Player declares Intent.

↓

Step 2

Player explains Approach.

↓

Step 3

Game Master determines whether a roll is necessary.

↓

Step 4

Game Master selects:

- Attribute
- Skill
- Difficulty

↓

Step 5

Roll Dice.

↓

Step 6

Interpret Results.

↓

Step 7

Narrate Consequences.

---

# Intent

Intent answers:

"What does the character actually want?"

Examples

"I want to quietly enter the warehouse."

"I want to calm the frightened villagers."

"I want to repair the damaged rudder."

Intent determines which Skill is appropriate.

---

# Approach

Approach answers:

"How does the character attempt it?"

Examples

Negotiation

Threats

Bribery

Stealth

Disguise

Force

Observation

Magic does not exist within the One Piece world.

Only established Engine systems may influence the outcome.

the Game Master selects the most relevant one.

Shipwright

Because the task concerns naval engineering.

# Skill Specialization System

The Skill Specialization System represents advanced expertise within a broader Skill.

A Specialization narrows a character's focus into a particular field of mastery.

Specializations do not replace Skills.

Instead, they represent exceptional knowledge gained through dedicated practice.

---

# Design Philosophy

A Skill answers:

"What can this character do?"

A Specialization answers:

"What does this character do exceptionally well?"

Every Specialization should deepen a Skill rather than create a new one.

---

# Purpose

The system exists to:

- Differentiate experienced characters.
- Reward long-term play.
- Encourage unique character identities.
- Avoid an excessive number of Skills.
- Support meaningful career development.

---

# Eligibility

A character may pursue a Specialization only after demonstrating significant experience with its parent Skill.

Requirements are determined by:

- Narrative achievements
- Training
- Practice
- Mentorship
- Story progression

A numerical Skill Rating alone is never sufficient.

---

# Choosing a Specialization

A character chooses a Specialization that logically fits:

- Background
- Occupation
- Training
- Personal history
- Campaign events

The Game Master approves all Specializations.

---

# Number of Specializations

Characters should possess relatively few Specializations.

Breadth creates versatility.

Specialization creates mastery.

Both approaches are valid.

---

# Changing Specializations

Knowledge cannot simply be forgotten.

A character may develop new Specializations over time.

Older Specializations remain part of the character's experience unless extraordinary narrative events occur.

# General Skill Rules

The following rules apply to every Skill within the Narrative Simulation Engine.

These rules override any individual Skill description unless explicitly stated otherwise.

---

## Rule 1

Skills represent learned capability.

They never represent talent alone.

---

## Rule 2

Attributes and Skills are always evaluated together whenever appropriate.

Neither system replaces the other.

---

## Rule 3

Skills may improve.

Attributes rarely do.

---

## Rule 4

Characters are allowed to attempt unfamiliar Skills unless the task requires specialized knowledge.

Attempting is not the same as succeeding.

---

## Rule 5

Professional knowledge cannot be improvised.

Certain tasks always require proper education or training.

Examples include:

- Major surgery
- Naval architecture
- Cannon engineering
- Advanced chemistry
- Government cryptography

---

## Rule 6

Narrative justification always takes priority over mechanical optimization.

If a character has never encountered a concept,

they cannot suddenly demonstrate expertise.

---

## Rule 7

Repeated identical attempts should not create repeated opportunities.

The situation must meaningfully change before another attempt is allowed.

---

# Skill and Attribute Guidelines

Selecting the correct Skill and Attribute is one of the Game Master's most important responsibilities.

The following principles should be followed consistently.

---

## Natural Ability

If success depends mostly on physical or mental potential,

the Attribute carries greater importance.

Examples:

Holding a collapsing beam.

Swimming for several hours.

Remembering ancient knowledge.

---

## Learned Ability

If success depends mostly upon education or practice,

the Skill carries greater importance.

Examples:

Performing surgery.

Repairing a rudder.

Negotiating a treaty.

---

## Mixed Situations

Most actions require both.

Examples:

Sword Duel

↓

+

Swordsmanship

Tracking Criminals

↓

Awareness

+

Tracking

Emergency Medical Treatment

↓

Intelligence

+

Medicine

# Improvised Solutions

Creative problem solving should always be encouraged.

Examples:

Using cooking oil as lubrication.

Repairing equipment with spare ship parts.

Treating injuries using improvised splints.

Using sails to slow a falling object.

Improvisation may reduce the need for difficult Skill Checks.

---

# Skill Limits

Possessing a Skill never guarantees:

Authority.

Permission.

Resources.

Opportunity.

Examples:

A doctor cannot perform surgery without patients.

A navigator cannot sail without a vessel.

A shipwright cannot build ships without materials.

The world determines possibility.

---

# Failure Guidelines

When a Skill Check fails,

the Game Master should prefer consequences over denial.

Common consequences include:

Time Loss.

Noise.

Resource Consumption.

Equipment Damage.

Attention.

Fatigue.

Partial Completion.

Complications.

Failure should expand the story rather than end it.

---

# Success Guidelines

Success should produce believable outcomes.

Benefits may include:

Completion.

Higher quality.

Improved efficiency.

Greater trust.

Reduced costs.

Additional opportunities.

Unexpected discoveries.

Success should never ignore established world logic.

---

# Skill Design Standards

Future Skills added to the Engine should follow these principles.

Every Skill must:

Represent learned experience.

Be broadly applicable.

Avoid overlap with existing Skills.

Remain useful throughout a campaign.

Support narrative gameplay.

Avoid unnecessary complexity.

If a proposed Skill can reasonably exist as a Specialization,

it should not become a new Skill.

# TRAIT SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Trait System defines the enduring positive characteristics that shape a character's behavior, decision-making, and interactions with the world.

Traits represent tendencies rather than abilities.

They describe who a character is, not what a character can do.

Traits provide narrative identity while supporting believable roleplaying throughout the campaign.

---

# Design Philosophy

Two characters with identical Attributes and Skills should still feel completely different.

Traits create individuality.

They influence:

- Personality
- Decision Making
- Habits
- Emotional Responses
- Social Interactions
- Leadership Style
- Problem Solving

Traits never remove player agency.

The player always chooses how their character acts.

Traits simply establish what comes naturally.

---

# Trait Definition

A Trait is a persistent positive characteristic.

Traits are not temporary emotions.

Traits are not supernatural powers.

Traits are not Skills.

Traits describe consistent patterns of behavior.

---

# Character Creation

Every playable character begins with one or more Traits during Character Creation.

Traits should logically match:

- Background
- Childhood
- Education
- Occupation
- Personal History

Traits should never be selected solely for mechanical advantage.

---

# Narrative Role

Traits help answer questions such as:

How does this character react under pressure?

How do they treat strangers?

What kind of leader are they?

What habits define them?

How do other people remember them?

---

---

# Consistency

Players are encouraged to roleplay consistently with their chosen Traits.

Occasional exceptions are normal.

Persistent contradictions should be explained through character development.

---

# Trait Synergy

Traits naturally interact with other systems.

Examples:

Calm

↓

Better emotional control during negotiations.

Observant

↓

More believable investigative behavior.

Compassionate

↓

Stronger patient relationships.

Disciplined

↓

More effective long-term training.

Trait synergy should always remain narrative first.

---

# Trait Development

Traits are generally stable.

However,

Major life events may gradually strengthen,

weaken,

or transform them.

Examples:

A fearless sailor becoming cautious after disaster.

A selfish merchant learning generosity.

A quiet recruit becoming an inspiring captain.

Such changes should occur through meaningful narrative progression.

---

# Replacing Traits

Replacing a Trait should be rare.

Requirements include:

Major character development.

Long-term story progression.

Significant emotional events.

Game Master approval.

Traits should never change simply to gain mechanical advantage.

---

# Hidden Traits

NPCs possess their own Traits.

Players discover them through:

Conversation.

Observation.

Shared experiences.

Reputation.

No character immediately knows another person's true nature.

---

# Trait Conflicts

Characters may possess Traits that create internal tension.

Examples:

Brave

+

Patient

Curious

+

Cautious

Kind

+

Strict

Logical

+

Compassionate

Conflicting Traits produce interesting roleplaying opportunities.

---

# Trait and Skill

Traits never replace Skills.

Example:

Being Curious does not grant Investigation.

Being Brave does not improve Swordsmanship.

Being Organized does not create Engineering knowledge.

Traits influence behavior.

Skills determine capability.

---

# Trait and Attribute

Traits are independent of Attributes.

A physically weak character may still be Courageous.

A highly intelligent character may still be Humble.

A charismatic leader may still be Patient.

The systems remain separate.

---

# Trait and Reputation

Consistently demonstrated Traits gradually shape Reputation.

Examples:

Reliable workers become trusted.

Honest merchants gain loyal customers.

Compassionate doctors become respected.

Cruel captains become feared.

Reputation reflects actions,

not Trait selection alone.

---

# Trait and Relationships

Traits strongly influence long-term relationships.

NPCs remember patterns.

Repeated demonstrations of kindness,

discipline,

or fairness affect future interactions.

Trust develops through consistency.

---

# Game Master Guidelines

Traits should be referenced naturally.

The Game Master should never force players to act according to their Traits.

Instead,

Traits provide context for:

NPC reactions.

Story opportunities.

Relationship development.

Personal growth.

---

# Future Expansion

Future systems may introduce:

Cultural Traits.

Regional Traits.

Occupational Traits.

Faction Traits.

Inherited Family Traits.

All additions must remain compatible with the Core Trait System.

---

# Engine Principles

The Trait System follows:

Story Before System.

Narrative Simulation.

Hidden But Fair.

World Before Player.

No Plot Armor.

Traits define the character's nature.

Actions define the character's legacy.

---
