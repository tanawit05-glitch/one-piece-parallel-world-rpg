# SKILL SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Skill System defines the learned abilities of every character within the Narrative Simulation Engine.

Unlike Attributes, Skills represent knowledge, training, practice, and experience gained throughout life.

Skills are the primary method by which characters improve over time.

Every meaningful profession, combat style, craft, and specialization is represented through Skills.

---

# Design Philosophy

Attributes determine natural potential.

Skills determine trained capability.

A naturally talented individual without training can still be surpassed by someone with years of experience.

The engine rewards learning rather than innate superiority.

---

# Skill Principles

Skills represent:

- Education
- Practice
- Professional Experience
- Repeated Success
- Repeated Failure
- Muscle Memory
- Technical Knowledge

Skills never represent supernatural power.

Devil Fruits and Haki are independent systems.

---

# Skill Categories

Every Skill belongs to one primary category.

Categories organize similar abilities but provide no mechanical bonus.

Categories include:

- Combat

- Survival

Most Skills begin at Rating 0.

# Skill Resolution System

The Skill Resolution System defines how Skills are evaluated during gameplay.

Every Skill Check follows the same fundamental process regardless of the Skill involved.

Consistency is more important than speed.

The Game Master always applies the same reasoning to similar situations.

---

# Resolution Philosophy

A Skill Check answers one question:

"Can this character successfully accomplish this action under the current circumstances?"

Skill Checks are not performed simply because a player attempts an action.

Dice are only introduced when:

- Success is uncertain.
- Failure is meaningful.
- Multiple outcomes are possible.

If success or failure is obvious, no roll is required.

---

# Resolution Sequence

Every Skill Check follows the same sequence.

Step 1

Player declares Intent.

↓

Step 2

Player explains Approach.

↓

Step 3

Game Master determines whether a roll is necessary.

↓

Step 4

Game Master selects:

- Attribute
- Skill
- Difficulty

↓

Step 5

Roll Dice.

↓

Step 6

Interpret Results.

↓

Step 7

Narrate Consequences.

---

# Intent

Intent answers:

"What does the character actually want?"

Examples

"I want to quietly enter the warehouse."

"I want to calm the frightened villagers."

"I want to repair the damaged rudder."

Intent determines which Skill is appropriate.

---

# Approach

Approach answers:

"How does the character attempt it?"

Examples

Negotiation

Threats

Bribery

Stealth

Disguise

Force

Observation

Magic does not exist within the One Piece world.

Only established Engine systems may influence the outcome.

---

# Selecting the Skill

The selected Skill should represent the primary trained ability required.

If multiple Skills appear valid,

the Game Master selects the most relevant one.

Example

Repairing a ship

Primary Skill

Shipwright

Not Carpentry

Because the task concerns naval engineering.

---

# Selecting the Attribute

Attributes represent natural ability.

Skills represent learned ability.

Both should logically support the attempted action.

Examples

Medical Surgery

Intelligence + Medicine

Tracking Criminals

Awareness + Tracking

Running Across Rooftops

Endurance + Athletics

Convincing Pirates

Presence + Persuasion

---

# Difficulty Selection

Difficulty depends upon the world,

never the player's experience.

Breaking an old wooden crate remains Easy,

whether attempted by a beginner or a master.

Likewise,

opening an advanced Government vault remains Extremely Difficult,

even for an expert.

# Skill Progression System

The Skill Progression System governs how characters improve learned abilities throughout the campaign.

Skill growth reflects meaningful experience gained through action, training, hardship, and personal development.

Time alone does not produce mastery.

Repeated meaningful practice does.

---

# Progression Philosophy

Characters improve because they:

- Practice consistently.
- Learn from mistakes.
- Receive proper instruction.
- Overcome increasingly difficult challenges.
- Accumulate real-world experience.

Experience earned through genuine narrative play is always valued above repetitive grinding.

---

# What Improves Skills

Skills may improve through:

- Active use
- Formal education
- Professional work
- Mentorship
- Research
- Field experience
- Personal experimentation
- Long-term training

No single method guarantees improvement.

The Game Master evaluates overall narrative progress.

---

# Meaningful Use

Not every use contributes to advancement.

Meaningful use generally includes:

- Difficult situations
- High-risk decisions
- First-time experiences
- Creative problem solving
- Significant responsibility
- Successful adaptation
- Learning from failure

Routine repetition eventually provides diminishing educational value.

---

# Training

Training is a legitimate form of advancement.

Training requires appropriate conditions.

Typical requirements include:

- Time
- Physical condition
- Proper equipment
- Safe environment
- Motivation

Training alone cannot instantly create mastery.

---

# Mentorship

A qualified mentor may significantly improve learning efficiency.

Good mentors provide:

- Correct techniques
- Feedback
- Experience
- Practical knowledge

Poor mentors may teach:

- Inefficient habits
- Incorrect information
- Dangerous methods

Not every teacher is equally skilled.

---

# Self-Learning

Characters may learn independently.

Examples include:

- Observation
- Books
- Trial and error
- Experimentation
- Practice

Self-learning generally progresses more slowly than expert instruction.

---

# Learning Through Failure

Failure is one of the greatest teachers.

Characters who survive mistakes often gain valuable experience.

Examples:

A failed surgery teaches anatomy.

A failed negotiation teaches diplomacy.

A failed voyage teaches navigation.

Failure should create opportunities for growth.

---

# Skill Plateaus

Improvement naturally slows as mastery increases.

Higher ratings require:

Greater effort.

Greater understanding.

Greater challenges.

Advancing from Expert to Master is far more difficult than advancing from Beginner to Competent.

---

# Knowledge Decay

Most Skills do not permanently disappear.

However,

Long periods without practice may reduce practical efficiency.

The Game Master may represent this through narrative disadvantages rather than reducing Skill Ratings.

# Skill Specialization System

The Skill Specialization System represents advanced expertise within a broader Skill.

A Specialization narrows a character's focus into a particular field of mastery.

Specializations do not replace Skills.

Instead, they represent exceptional knowledge gained through dedicated practice.

---

# Design Philosophy

A Skill answers:

"What can this character do?"

A Specialization answers:

"What does this character do exceptionally well?"

Every Specialization should deepen a Skill rather than create a new one.

---

# Purpose

The system exists to:

- Differentiate experienced characters.
- Reward long-term play.
- Encourage unique character identities.
- Avoid an excessive number of Skills.
- Support meaningful career development.

---

# Eligibility

A character may pursue a Specialization only after demonstrating significant experience with its parent Skill.

Requirements are determined by:

- Narrative achievements
- Training
- Practice
- Mentorship
- Story progression

A numerical Skill Rating alone is never sufficient.

---

# Choosing a Specialization

A character chooses a Specialization that logically fits:

- Background
- Occupation
- Training
- Personal history
- Campaign events

The Game Master approves all Specializations.

---

# Number of Specializations

Characters should possess relatively few Specializations.

Breadth creates versatility.

Specialization creates mastery.

Both approaches are valid.

---

# Changing Specializations

Knowledge cannot simply be forgotten.

A character may develop new Specializations over time.

Older Specializations remain part of the character's experience unless extraordinary narrative events occur.

---

# Multiple Specializations

Examples include:

# Narrative Benefits

Specializations may provide:

- Greater efficiency.
- Reduced Difficulty.
- Faster completion.
- Better quality.
- Unique narrative opportunities.
- Increased professional recognition.

Specializations never remove the possibility of failure.

---

# Professional Recognition

Well-known specialists may develop reputations.

Examples:

Master Shipwright

Famous Surgeon

Renowned Navigator

Legendary Chef

Professional recognition exists within the world and influences NPC behavior.

---

# Hidden Expertise

NPCs rarely know a character's Specializations without prior interaction.

Reputation develops naturally through:

Work.

Success.

Stories.

Witnesses.

News.

---

# Improvisation Outside Specialization

Characters may still use the parent Skill outside their area of expertise.

Specialization represents exceptional competence,

not exclusivity.

---

# Game Master Authority

The Game Master determines:

Whether a proposed Specialization fits.

Whether sufficient narrative justification exists.

Whether benefits apply in a given situation.

Consistency is more important than generosity.

# General Skill Rules

The following rules apply to every Skill within the Narrative Simulation Engine.

These rules override any individual Skill description unless explicitly stated otherwise.

---

## Rule 1

Skills represent learned capability.

They never represent talent alone.

---

## Rule 2

Attributes and Skills are always evaluated together whenever appropriate.

Neither system replaces the other.

---

## Rule 3

Skills may improve.

Attributes rarely do.

---

## Rule 4

Characters are allowed to attempt unfamiliar Skills unless the task requires specialized knowledge.

Attempting is not the same as succeeding.

---

## Rule 5

Professional knowledge cannot be improvised.

Certain tasks always require proper education or training.

Examples include:

- Major surgery
- Naval architecture
- Cannon engineering
- Advanced chemistry
- Government cryptography

---

## Rule 6

Narrative justification always takes priority over mechanical optimization.

If a character has never encountered a concept,

they cannot suddenly demonstrate expertise.

---

## Rule 7

Repeated identical attempts should not create repeated opportunities.

The situation must meaningfully change before another attempt is allowed.

---

# Skill and Attribute Guidelines

Selecting the correct Skill and Attribute is one of the Game Master's most important responsibilities.

The following principles should be followed consistently.

---

## Natural Ability

If success depends mostly on physical or mental potential,

the Attribute carries greater importance.

Examples:

Holding a collapsing beam.

Swimming for several hours.

Remembering ancient knowledge.

---

## Learned Ability

If success depends mostly upon education or practice,

the Skill carries greater importance.

Examples:

Performing surgery.

Repairing a rudder.

Negotiating a treaty.

---

## Mixed Situations

Most actions require both.

Examples:

Sword Duel

↓

Agility

+

Swordsmanship

Tracking Criminals

↓

Awareness

+

Tracking

Emergency Medical Treatment

↓

Intelligence

+

Medicine

---

# Multiple Valid Skills

Some situations may reasonably support different Skills.

Example

Opening a secured building.

Possible approaches:

Lockpicking

Breaking the Door

Climbing Through a Window

Forging Entry Papers

Bribing the Guard

Each approach uses different Skills.

Creativity should be rewarded.

---

# Environmental Influence

Skills never exist in isolation.

The Game Master should always consider:

Weather.

Terrain.

Visibility.

Noise.

Crowds.

Fatigue.

Stress.

Equipment.

Available time.

Narrative circumstances.

The world constantly influences outcomes.

# NPC Skills

NPCs follow the same Skill framework as players.

Major NPCs possess complete Skill profiles.

Minor NPCs may use simplified Skill summaries.

The same rules apply equally to everyone.

The world does not distinguish between player and non-player characters.

---

# Hidden NPC Information

NPC Skill Ratings remain hidden.

Players discover expertise through:

Observation.

Conversation.

Reputation.

Experience.

Investigation.

The engine discourages numerical knowledge.

---

# Improvised Solutions

Creative problem solving should always be encouraged.

Examples:

Using cooking oil as lubrication.

Repairing equipment with spare ship parts.

Treating injuries using improvised splints.

Using sails to slow a falling object.

Improvisation may reduce the need for difficult Skill Checks.

---

# Skill Limits

Possessing a Skill never guarantees:

Authority.

Permission.

Resources.

Opportunity.

Examples:

A doctor cannot perform surgery without patients.

A navigator cannot sail without a vessel.

A shipwright cannot build ships without materials.

The world determines possibility.

---

# Failure Guidelines

When a Skill Check fails,

the Game Master should prefer consequences over denial.

Common consequences include:

Time Loss.

Noise.

Resource Consumption.

Equipment Damage.

Attention.

Fatigue.

Partial Completion.

Complications.

Failure should expand the story rather than end it.

---

# Success Guidelines

Success should produce believable outcomes.

Benefits may include:

Completion.

Higher quality.

Improved efficiency.

Greater trust.

Reduced costs.

Additional opportunities.

Unexpected discoveries.

Success should never ignore established world logic.

---

# Skill Design Standards

Future Skills added to the Engine should follow these principles.

Every Skill must:

Represent learned experience.

Be broadly applicable.

Avoid overlap with existing Skills.

Remain useful throughout a campaign.

Support narrative gameplay.

Avoid unnecessary complexity.

If a proposed Skill can reasonably exist as a Specialization,

it should not become a new Skill.

---

## Skill

## Specialization

## Skill Check

A resolution using one or more Skills.

---

## Passive Skill

A Skill that may function without player declaration.

---

## Active Skill

A Skill requiring intentional use.

---

## Opposed Check

Two or more characters directly competing through Skills.

---

## Lead Character

The primary character making a cooperative Skill Check.

---

## Supporting Character

A character assisting another during a Skill Check.

---

## Meaningful Practice

Training or experience that contributes to long-term improvement.
