# REPUTATION SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Reputation System tracks how the world perceives a character.

Reputation is earned through actions, achievements, failures, rumors, and the spread of information.

It exists independently of the character's personal beliefs.

A character may believe themselves to be a hero while the world views them as a criminal.

Likewise, a feared pirate may secretly perform acts of kindness unknown to the public.

Reputation represents perception, not truth.

---

# Design Philosophy

The world remembers.

People talk.

Witnesses survive.

Newspapers publish stories.

Governments investigate.

Pirates exaggerate.

Rumors spread.

Every meaningful action leaves an impression.

Reputation ensures that actions continue to influence the world long after they occur.

---

# Reputation Principles

Reputation is:

- Earned.
- Dynamic.
- Localized.
- Imperfect.
- Persistent.

Reputation is never assigned simply because the player desires it.

---

# Sources of Reputation

A character's Reputation may change through:

- Heroic actions.
- Criminal activity.
- Public speeches.
- Professional excellence.
- Acts of cruelty.
- Rescue missions.
- Military victories.
- Political influence.
- Scientific discoveries.
- Exploration.
- Journalism.
- Commerce.
- Rumors.
- Betrayal.
- Sacrifice.

Both success and failure may affect Reputation.

---

# Public Knowledge

The public rarely knows the complete truth.

Instead,

people remember stories.

Examples:

"The captain defeated three pirate crews."

"The doctor saved an entire village."

"The merchant cheated the governor."

Whether these stories are accurate is often irrelevant.

Public perception shapes Reputation.

---

# Reputation Scale

Reputation exists on a narrative scale rather than a fixed numerical value.

Common stages include:

Unknown

↓

Recognized

↓

Well Known

↓

Famous

↓

Renowned

↓

Legendary

Recognition may be positive,

negative,

or mixed.

---

# Local Reputation

Reputation is regional.

A hero in East Blue may be completely unknown in Paradise.

Likewise,

a notorious pirate in the New World may attract little attention in a remote village.

Information spreads gradually.

---

# Professional Reputation

Professional Reputation develops independently.

Examples:

A famous surgeon.

A respected navigator.

A renowned shipwright.

A legendary chef.

Professional reputation creates opportunities within the character's field.

---

# Criminal Reputation

Illegal actions may create Criminal Reputation.

Examples:

Piracy.

Smuggling.

Assassination.

Fraud.

Prison escape.

Government opposition.

Criminal Reputation attracts attention from law enforcement and rival criminals alike.

---

# Government Reputation

Governments maintain records on individuals of interest.

Records may include:

Criminal history.

Military service.

Professional licenses.

Political activity.

Known associates.

These records are not always public.

---

# Faction Reputation

Each Faction forms its own opinion of a character.

Examples:

Marines.

World Government.

Pirate Crews.

Revolutionary Army.

Merchant Guilds.

Independent Nations.

Improving Reputation with one Faction may reduce it with another.

Relationships between Factions influence these changes.

---

# Personal Reputation

NPCs remember personal experiences.

Examples:

Promises kept.

Promises broken.

Acts of kindness.

Acts of cruelty.

Professional competence.

Leadership.

Personal Reputation spreads slowly but remains highly reliable.

---

# Reputation Spread

Information spreads through:

Witnesses.

Travelers.

Merchants.

Newspapers.

Den Den Mushi.

Government Reports.

Pirate Rumors.

Word of mouth.

The speed of information depends upon:

Distance.

Importance.

Political interest.

Media coverage.

Communication networks.

---

# False Reputation

Not all Reputation is accurate.

Examples:

Mistaken identity.

False accusations.

Deliberate propaganda.

Exaggerated stories.

Hidden heroism.

Characters may attempt to correct false Reputation,

but success is never guaranteed.

---

# Hidden Actions

Actions without witnesses rarely affect Reputation.

Secret operations remain secret unless evidence later emerges.

Knowledge and Reputation are separate concepts.

---

# Reputation Decay

The world gradually forgets.

Minor accomplishments fade over time.

Extraordinary achievements remain part of history.

Recent events usually influence public opinion more strongly than distant memories.

---

# Reputation Recovery

Damaged Reputation may be repaired.

Recovery requires:

Time.

Consistent behavior.

Public actions.

Credible witnesses.

Narrative effort.

Trust rebuilds slowly.

---

# Reputation and Opportunities

Reputation influences:

Employment.

Trade.

Leadership.

Negotiations.

Hospitality.

Political access.

Recruitment.

Education.

Travel.

High Reputation opens doors.

Poor Reputation closes others.

Neither guarantees success.

---

# Reputation and Fear

Some characters become feared rather than respected.

Fear produces different reactions.

Possible responses include:

Avoidance.

Submission.

Hostility.

Preparation.

Betrayal.

Fear is powerful,

but unstable.

---

# Reputation and Trust

Trust is personal.

Reputation is public.

A famous hero may still be distrusted by an individual.

An infamous pirate may be deeply trusted by loyal allies.

These systems remain separate.

---

# Reputation and Bounties

For criminals,

Reputation often contributes to Bounty growth.

However,

Bounties are determined by the Government,

not solely by public opinion.

Political factors may outweigh actual danger.

The Bounty System defines official bounty calculations.

---

# Reputation and Newspapers

Major events may appear in newspapers.

Publication depends upon:

Newsworthiness.

Political censorship.

Journalistic access.

Editorial priorities.

Not every important event reaches the front page.

---

# Reputation and Character Growth

Reputation changes how the world responds.

It does not change who the character is.

Characters remain free to embrace,

ignore,

or reject their public image.

---

# NPC Reputation

NPCs possess Reputation just like players.

Famous doctors,

pirate captains,

Marine officers,

scientists,

and merchants all develop public identities over time.

The same rules apply equally.

---

# Game Master Guidelines

The Game Master should use Reputation to:

Create believable NPC reactions.

Generate opportunities.

Introduce rivals.

Influence negotiations.

Support world continuity.

Reflect long-term consequences.

Reputation should emerge naturally from play.

Never award or remove Reputation arbitrarily.

---

# Future Expansion

Future systems may introduce:

National Reputation.

Crew Reputation.

Fleet Reputation.

Political Reputation.

Scientific Reputation.

Military Reputation.

All expansions must remain compatible with the Core Reputation System.

---

# Engine Principles

The Reputation System follows:

Story Before System.

Narrative Simulation.

Hidden But Fair.

World Before Player.

No Plot Armor.

A reputation is not built by words.

It is built by the stories the world chooses to remember.

---

# End of Reputation System
