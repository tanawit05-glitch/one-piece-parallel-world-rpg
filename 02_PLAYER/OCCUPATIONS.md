# OCCUPATION SYSTEM

---

# Purpose

The Occupation System defines a character's primary profession before and during the campaign.

An Occupation represents the practical application of a character's knowledge, training, and experience.

Occupations provide narrative identity and establish a character's role within society.

An Occupation is not a class.

It does not restrict character growth.

---

# Design Philosophy

Occupation answers one question:

"What does this character do?"

An Occupation reflects experience,

not destiny.

Characters may:

- Change careers.
- Learn additional professions.
- Abandon former occupations.
- Develop multiple areas of expertise.

The world values demonstrated ability over job titles.

---

# Narrative Role

Occupations influence:

- Daily life.
- Income.
- Social status.
- Professional reputation.
- Available opportunities.
- Existing relationships.
- Practical experience.

Occupation should naturally explain why a character possesses certain Skills.

---

# Occupation During Character Creation

Every playable character begins with one Primary Occupation.

The chosen Occupation should logically fit:

- Background
- Education
- Skills
- Personal History

The Game Master approves unusual combinations.

---

# Occupation Components

Every Occupation defines:

- Profession
- Typical Duties
- Required Skills
- Common Equipment
- Professional Knowledge
- Working Environment
- Social Expectations
- Advancement Opportunities

---

# Professional Knowledge

Each Occupation grants knowledge expected from someone working within that profession.

Examples:

A doctor understands common diseases.

A navigator understands charts.

A carpenter recognizes wood quality.

A merchant understands market prices.

Professional knowledge does not guarantee success.

It allows believable participation.

# Income

Occupation influences a character's normal source of income.

Income depends upon:

Demand

Experience

Location

Economy

Political conditions

Personal reputation

Occupation alone never guarantees wealth.

---

# Social Standing

Different societies value occupations differently.

Examples:

Doctors may be respected.

Pirates may be feared.

Merchants may be welcomed.

Government officials may possess authority.

Criminal professions may require secrecy.

Social status always depends upon local culture.

---

# Career Progression

Occupations naturally evolve.

Examples:

Apprentice

↓

Worker

↓

Professional

↓

Senior Professional

↓

Master

↓

Recognized Authority

Progression follows narrative achievement,

not automatic promotion.

---

# Career Change

Characters may change Occupations.

Requirements include:

Training.

Experience.

Opportunity.

Narrative justification.

Changing Occupation never erases previous experience.

Former professions remain part of the character's history.

---

# Multiple Occupations

Some characters possess more than one profession.

Examples:

Doctor and Researcher.

Merchant and Navigator.

Shipwright and Engineer.

Hunter and Guide.

Each Occupation should remain believable.

The Game Master determines whether sufficient experience exists.

---

# Professional Reputation

Professional reputation develops independently from general reputation.

Examples:

A famous surgeon.

A respected shipwright.

A notorious smuggler.

A legendary chef.

Professional reputation spreads through:

Clients.

Witnesses.

News.

Recommendations.

Professional organizations.

---

# Professional Ethics

Certain Occupations possess ethical expectations.

Examples:

Doctors protect life.

Marines obey orders.

Judges seek justice.

Journalists report information.

Merchants honor agreements.

Characters may violate these expectations,

but doing so may influence Reputation and Relationships.

---

# Occupational Hazards

Every profession carries risks.

Examples:

Doctors face disease.

Sailors face storms.

Hunters face wildlife.

Blacksmiths face burns.

Merchants face robbery.

Hazards help shape believable narratives.

---

# Retirement

Characters may retire from their profession.

Retirement changes daily life,

but previous knowledge remains.

Professional reputation often survives retirement.

---

# Occupation Loss

A character may lose the ability to continue a profession.

Examples:

Career-ending injury.

Political exile.

Destroyed business.

Loss of license.

Public disgrace.

Occupation loss creates new narrative opportunities.

# Game Master Guidelines

Occupations should be used to:

Generate work opportunities.

Create NPC contacts.

Introduce contracts.

Provide economic motivation.

Develop long-term stories.

Occupation should remain relevant throughout the campaign.

---

# Future Expansion

Additional Occupations may be introduced through future Engine modules.

Every new Occupation must:

Support the existing Skill System.

Remain narratively meaningful.

Avoid duplication.

Fit naturally within the One Piece world.

---

# Engine Principles

The Occupation System follows:

Story Before System.

Narrative Simulation.

Hidden But Fair.

World Before Player.

No Plot Armor.

A profession is more than a source of income.

It is a reflection of experience,

responsibility,

and the life a character has chosen to live.
